When an App Has to Ask “Are You Dead?”

What a viral app reveals about loneliness and the need to be seen

Joe Lovett & Khushi Yadav February 11, 2026

A recent CNN article caught my attention.

It described a viral app in China that asks people who live alone to check in every day.
If they don’t, it alerts an emergency contact.

The app isn’t popular because of clever technology.
It’s popular because it taps into something deeper.

Loneliness. Isolation. The fear of disappearing unnoticed.

One user put it bluntly:
“The scariest thing isn’t loneliness. It’s disappearing.”

What’s striking is not the app itself, but what it reveals.

People aren’t just looking for safety.
They’re looking for signs that someone sees them.

A professor quoted in the article said the app “taps into this feeling of atomization, being stuck on your own, being isolated in terms of very long working hours. ”

In essence, the app can help in moments of crisis, but we can all agree, should never substitute for meaningful human interaction.

At Hoogah, we think about connection long before it reaches an emergency state.

Belonging doesn’t start with alerts.
It starts with conversation.
With belonging.
With being paired intentionally.
With having moments where you’re not just present, but noticed.

Technology can help check if someone is alive.
It can also help us make sure people feel alive together.

The question isn’t whether we’re connected enough to be safe.

It’s whether we’re connected enough to belong.
