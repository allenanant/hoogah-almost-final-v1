Why Training Is One of the Most Overlooked Culture Levers

And it doesn’t require massive programs or complex infrastructure

Joe Lovett & Khushi Yadav January 21, 2026

Where we’re currently building connection

2026 is shaping up to be the year organizations take employee well-being seriously. Not as a perk or a program, but as a core leadership responsibility.

Recent research and reporting point to a shift. Well-being at work is no longer defined only by benefits, flexibility, or mental health support. It increasingly includes whether people feel connected, supported, and part of something meaningful at work.

Yet one of the most powerful tools companies already have to support that sense of connection is rarely discussed in this context. Training.

Most organizations think of training as a way to transfer skills, close gaps, or improve performance. Those outcomes matter. But they are only part of the story.

Training also plays a quiet but critical role in how people experience culture.

The Current State of Corporate Training

In many companies today, training is treated as a transactional function.

People attend sessions, complete modules, earn certificates of completion, then they check the box and move on.

Think back to the last training you attended that actually stayed with you.

Probably not the workbook or the slides.
Maybe the trainer and their strange approach and props.

But it’s really about the people.

I remember one training program I participated in that lasted three intense days. Participants came from all over the country. We shared meals and drinks. Broke into small groups. Compared notes. Worked through real problems. We learned, struggled, and laughed together.

By the end of it, I felt closer to people I had just met than to many I worked with every day.

And then it ended.

Everyone went back to their day jobs. Their inboxes, routines, and daily fire drills.
The connection faded, even though the experience had been real.

That is what most training misses.

Corporate training is designed to impart knowledge and drive specific behaviors and outcomes. It prioritizes completion and efficiency, not connection.
Even when the learning content is strong, the experience often remains transactional.
Interaction exists, but is usually structured around tasks, not relationships.

That approach misses something important.

Training is one of the few moments where organizations intentionally bring people together around shared goals, shared language, and shared effort. When designed well, it becomes a collective experience rather than an individual task.

The Opportunity Training Creates

Well-being is not only about reducing stress or burnout. It is also about belonging.

People want to feel that they matter. That they are growing. That they are part of a larger mission and a real community.

Training can support all of that.

When organizations invest in learning, they signal trust and commitment.
Learning together builds shared experience and understanding.
When training connects skills to purpose, people see how their work fits into something bigger than their role.

Most importantly, when training creates space for conversation and reflection, it helps people see each other as humans, not just functions on an org chart.

Training as a Connector, Not Just a Classroom

The next evolution of corporate training is not more content. It is more intentional connection.

Training that builds culture is designed to create connection. It pairs people thoughtfully. It encourages discussion that goes beyond titles and transactions. It creates moments where people feel seen, heard, and valued.

This does not require massive programs or complex infrastructure.

It simply requires recognizing that learning is a social experience.

When people leave training feeling more connected to the mission and to each other, the impact lasts far longer than the material itself.

Where Hoogah Fits

In a year when well-being is becoming a leadership priority, training has the potential to do more than teach skills.

It can help people belong.
