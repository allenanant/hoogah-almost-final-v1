The Friendship Recession Is Real, and It’s Hurting Your Workplace

As work becomes more efficient, it's also becoming more isolating. Why building real connections at work isn't optional anymore.

Joe Lovett & Khushi Yadav October 22, 2025

A recent Fast Company article shared a stat that floored me: According to a recent Gallup poll, only 20% of employees have a “best friend” at work.

If you’ve ever had that person at work, the one you grab coffee with, commiserate with, or lean on when everything feels like it’s on fire, then you know how much they matter. They make the hard days bearable and the good days even better. They’re also the reason you’ll stay in a role longer than you might otherwise.

To punctuate the point, Gallup’s research shows that employees with close bonds are 43% more committed and 27% more satisfied in their jobs. Friendship isn’t a soft benefit. It’s a measurable driver of retention, performance, and loyalty.

But those friendships are vanishing.

Why We’re Losing Work Friends

The structure of work has changed. Hybrid schedules, flexible hours, and asynchronous collaboration tools have made teams more efficient, but also more transactional. We move from meeting to meeting, task to task, and message to message, often without space for the informal conversations that used to spark connection.

Even when colleagues are in the same office, they may work different days or keep different hours. The casual hallway chats and shared lunches that once built bonds have quietly disappeared. What’s left often feels like a thread of communication without the fabric of relationship.

At the same time, many organizations now reward individual performance over collective success. The implicit message is clear: focus on your output, not your connections. The result is a workplace that functions, but doesn’t always feel like a team.

Why This Matters More Than You Think

The Fast Company piece highlighted research from Oxford professor Jan-Emmanuel De Neve showing that people often leave jobs not because of bad managers, but because they don’t feel a sense of belonging with their team.

That’s huge.

We all know bad managers drive people out of companies, but a lack of belonging can push them just as quickly. It’s not just about leadership quality. It’s about whether people feel connected to those they work with day in and day out. Without that, motivation fades, creativity stalls, and people start to disengage.

And this isn’t just about retention. Friendship fuels collaboration, problem-solving, and resilience. It’s what turns a group of employees into a more productive community, and one that supports each other when things get hard.

Friendships Don’t Just “Happen” Anymore

Work friendships used to develop organically; through shared projects, offsite lunches, or post-meeting conversations. But as work has become more distributed and time-bound, those organic moments have been squeezed out.

The problem isn’t remote work or flexibility. It’s the transactional rhythm that now defines how most teams operate. Many leaders assume that because collaboration is happening, connection must be too. But collaboration without connection is just coordination.

That’s where intentional design comes in.
