The Room I Couldn't Own Anymore

When even the most social among us start to hesitate, it’s time to rethink how we build belonging.

Joe Lovett October 09, 2025

I come from a highly social family. My siblings affectionately call our extended family “aggressively social.” I was one of the most social. As they say the apple doesn’t fall far from the tree. Our family get-togethers could probably be heard from outer space. We filled rooms with conversation, laughter, and competing stories. Yes, we had a few in-laws who were more reserved, maybe even introverted, but the dominant energy was loud and lively.

Fast forward to COVID, and something shifted in me.

I first noticed it on a vacation in the spring of 2021, just after our family was fully vaccinated. We went to a ranch in Tucson, Arizona, one of those communal experiences where everyone eats together, watches evening shows, and rides horses side by side. Normally, that would have been my element. I would’ve made a few new friends before the first cattle wrangling.

But this time, I didn’t.

I smiled, nodded, exchanged small talk. But I didn’t lean in. I didn’t go deep. It wasn’t nerves. It wasn’t disinterest. It was more like a softness, a hesitance I hadn’t known before.

And I realized, something had changed.

When Connection Starts to Feel Harder

The truth is, even people who once thrived on connection are finding it harder. The world feels noisier, and yet personal connection feels thinner. COVID didn’t just distance us physically, it rewired how many of us show up.

We got used to fewer interactions. More boundaries.
And slowly, without noticing, many of us lost the ease we once had.

I used to think of connection as something that just happens. Now I see it as something we have to design for. Because if connection is harder for people like me, people who once owned the room, imagine how it feels for those who never felt at home in one to begin with.

The Case for Designed Connection

Whether it’s at a conference, in a company, club or community, the room has changed. People arrive carrying more than their lanyards and badges. They carry exhaustion, uncertainty, a quiet hope that maybe this time they’ll meet someone who they feel at ease with. Someone who ‘gets it.’

If we want to rebuild belonging, we can’t leave it to chance.
We have to design moments that make connection feel natural again.

Moments that say, “You’re welcome here.”
Moments that help people find their way in.

That’s what I’ve learned, not as a founder, but as someone still figuring out how to reconnect, too.
