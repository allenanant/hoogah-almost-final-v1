Why Events Need More Breathing Room (And What Most Agendas Get Wrong)

In a distracted, hybrid world, connection is the reset your attendees are starving for.

Joe Lovett December 10, 2025

But first… Hoogah In Action
Where we’re currently building connection

Events: 8
Communities: 4
Workshops and Training: 1
Employers: 0

Most conferences, offsites, SKOs, and town halls operate from a well-intended but flawed assumption:

If we have people together, we should maximize every minute.

Pack the breakout rooms. Extend the general sessions. Layer in more breakouts, more speakers, more panels.

On paper it feels productive.
In reality, it works against how humans actually operate, especially in today’s work environment.

The Modern Work Rhythm Has Changed

Hybrid and remote employees do not work traditional 9-5’s anymore.

A real day looks more like this:

Check emails before getting the kids up, crank out a few hours early in the morning, then errands, then a workout, then parenting or school pickups, then late night laptop time once the house is quiet.

People are not sitting still for eight uninterrupted hours. Yet we expect them to arrive at a three day event and suddenly behave like they do.

People Also Have Jobs They Cannot Escape

Even at offsites and conferences, attendees are managing:

• Urgent emails
• Fire drills
• Performance reviews
• Contract negotiations
• Leadership escalations

Whether we like it or not, they need breaks because they need to keep their actual jobs moving.

And Then There Is The Science Of Attention

Our brains cannot absorb back-to-back content blocks. Studies show that:

• Attention resets every 8 to 15 minutes
• Movement increases cognitive retention
• Changing settings improves recall and creativity
• Shifting your role from listener to contributor boosts engagement

Which means connection breaks are not fluffy agenda fillers.
They are performance infrastructure.

Networking actually fuels learning.

But Here Is Where Events Go Wrong

Most event breaks are:

• 5-10 minutes
• A line for coffee and pastries
• A mad dash to the bathroom
• A chance to answer a few emails

That is not a reset.
That is survival.

We need actual breathing room, designed for:

• Exploration
• Meaningful interaction
• Reflection
• Human conversation

Networking Should Not Be Added. It Should Be Designed.

Great agendas give people permission to connect, not just time to stand in a hallway.

That means creating:

Intentional 1:1s
Guided conversations
Topic based connection zones
Facilitated reflection
Pairing between people who should know each other

Because when people engage with each other, they re-engage with the content.

They move from consumer to contributor.

Their attention resets.
Their energy rises.
Their commitment strengthens.

The Event World Is In A Transition Moment

Work is fragmented.
Teams are dispersed.
Attention is thin.
Everyone is stretched.

Events might be the only place people feel part of something bigger than their inbox.

So why rush them from one session to the next?

Give people space.

Let the breaks do some of the work.
