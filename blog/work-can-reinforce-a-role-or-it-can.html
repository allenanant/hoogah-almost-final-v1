Work Can Reinforce a Role. Or It Can Reinforce a Person.

A recent Wall Street Journal article found that paid time off makes employees feel more “human” than bonuses. The real issue isn’t vacation. It’s identity.

Joe Lovett & Khushi Yadav March 11, 2026

A recent Wall Street Journal article highlighted research conducted by Sanford DeVoe of UCLA and his colleagues examining how employees feel when receiving bonuses versus paid time off.

Money vs. time, right?

The researchers framed it differently.

They measured how “human” employees felt after receiving each reward

On a seven-point scale, paid vacation scored higher than cash bonuses. The numerical difference wasn’t massive. But according to the study’s authors, it represented a meaningful psychological shift. It was the difference between feeling neutral and feeling genuinely seen as a person.

Role Identity vs. Human Identity

Most organizations, often unintentionally, reinforce role identity.

You are your title.
You are your output.
You are your quarterly performance.

Bonuses fit cleanly into this system. They reward and are tied to each of these.

Time off sends a different signal.

It acknowledges that you exist beyond the role. That you have a life outside the organization. That you are more than your deliverables.

Over time, it shapes how people experience work.

When Work Doesn’t Let Go

The study included another experiment.

Participants imagined being on vacation and receiving two texts. One from their mother or friend, the other from their boss.

When the second text came from a boss, the “feeling human” score dropped significantly.

That says something important.

Time off only works if work actually lets go.

If you are technically on vacation but mentally still on call, you are not fully away.

And people feel that.

Why This Matters

Most companies focus on incentives.

How do we motivate?
How do we reward?
How do we drive performance?

All valid questions.

But here’s another one:

Does our workplace reinforce role identity… or human identity?

Because when employees feel more human, the data shows higher engagement, productivity, and lower turnover.

Definitely not fluffy metrics.

Designing for the Whole Person

Time off is one lever.

But it’s not the only one.

Every organization sends signals about what matters most and most organizations reinforce role identity by default.

So the question becomes:

How do you start helping people feel more human at work?
How do you help them see one another beyond title and output?

We believe one way is intentional connection.

When people are paired outside their usual lanes, something shifts.

They are no longer just the marketing lead or the finance director, or even the CFO.

They are someone with context. Someone with interests. Someone navigating life just like the rest of us.

That changes the texture of work.
