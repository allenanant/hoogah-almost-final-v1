Why “Networking” Feels Broken, and What We’re Doing About It

We don’t need more networking. We need better ways to find the people who truly get what we’re building.

Joe Lovett November 13, 2025

I went to a founder meetup last week. The good news? It was absolutely packed, proof that the entrepreneurial spirit is alive and well.

The bad news? It was almost impossible to actually meet anyone.
We were human rivers in tightly packed flows. Every sponsor table was mobbed. Conversations were loud, brief, and mostly between people who already knew each other. I suppose it was networking, just without much connection.

These events are built with good intentions. We all show up wanting to connect. But between the crowds, the noise, and the surface-level conversations, most of us leave with a few business cards and the feeling we might’ve missed someone we were supposed to meet.

The Promise (and Problem) of Connection

Social media promised to bring us closer. Instead, it left us more separated, as people and as a culture.

Networking events are supposed to fix that. Yet they often replicate the same problem: everyone talking, few people truly connecting.

For introverts, it’s even tougher. Walking into a crowded room of strangers can feel overwhelming with too much noise and too little purpose.

In fact, most networking environments are designed for extroverts. The loudest voices win. The quietest ones fade. That’s not connection or how to build community.

Connection, the kind that sparks collaboration and belonging, rarely happens by accident. It happens by design.

How to Fix Networking

If we want networking to actually work, it starts with intention.

1. Connect with someone who actually wants to connect.
The best conversations happen when both people are open and curious, not just checking boxes or working the room.

2. Connect with people who share similar context.
When you meet someone with a shared background, goal, or stage of growth, the conversation feels natural. You skip the small talk and get to what matters.

3. Show people how they’re connected.
Every great connection starts with context; knowing what you have in common and why you’re talking. It turns an introduction into a shared starting point.
