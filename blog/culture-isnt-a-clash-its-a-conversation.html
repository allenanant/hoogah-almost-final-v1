Culture Isn’t a Clash. It’s a Conversation.

Why Gen Z’s “values mismatch” isn’t the problem. It’s the opportunity.

Joe Lovett & Khushi Yadav October 29, 2025

But first… Hoogah In Action
Where we’re currently building connection

Employers: 0

A recent Business Insider article explored the huge gap between Gen Z and hiring managers.

The research behind it, led by NYU professor Suzy Welch, revealed something striking. Employers value recognition, ambition, and work-centrism. Gen Z values authenticity, self-care, and helping others.

Two very different playbooks for what “success” means.

At first glance, it looks like a clash of priorities. But really, it’s a clash of connection, a gap in how we see and understand each other. Few organizations have created the space to actually talk about those differences.

The study found that only 2% of values overlapped between hiring managers and Gen Z employees.

Gen Z isn’t rejecting work. They’re redefining it.
They’re asking, “Can I succeed here without losing myself?”
Meanwhile, many managers are asking, “Can I count on this person to commit?”

Both are fair questions. But they’re being asked in isolation.

We’ve designed workplaces that prioritize efficiency and performance, but not conversation. We optimize workflows, automate communication, and measure everything except how well people understand one another.

And when understanding goes missing, culture starts to fracture.

Culture Isn’t a Clash. It’s a Conversation.

Different values aren’t a threat. They’re opportunities. Different perspectives are essential to problem solving and innovation. If we know how to surface them with curiosity instead of judgment.

When companies intentionally design space for honest, curiosity-driven dialog between management and employees, something powerful happens: shared meaning.

That’s what culture really is, the ongoing dialog (and, gasp, the evolution) between what the company values and what its people value.

The Hidden Cost of Silence

When that dialog doesn’t happen, connection breaks down.

Employees start to feel misunderstood. Leaders start to feel frustrated.
What was once curiosity turns into judgment.

We see it in hiring interviews that feel like mismatched dates.
In onboarding that talks about perks but not purpose.
In cultures where people quietly adapt their values to fit in, or quietly plan their exit.

As Welch put it, “You don’t want to hire the 98% and then slap your values on them and wonder why everybody’s miserable.”
In other words, you can’t build belonging on forced alignment. You have to start with understanding. After all, belonging isn’t built by compliance. It’s built by connection.

The Takeaway

The Gen Z “values gap” isn’t a generational divide. It’s a communication divide.

Work has changed, but the human need for connection hasn’t.
And the companies that treat culture as an ongoing conversation, not a one-time alignment workshop, will be the ones people want to stay with and grow in.
